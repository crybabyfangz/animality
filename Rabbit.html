RABBIT

The rabbit response emerges when instability is interpreted primarily as threat. The system does not engage with problems directly; instead it prioritizes escape, distance, or avoidance of immediate confrontation. Stability is defined by absence of perceived danger rather than resolution of the issue itself.

Perception becomes hyper-sensitive to signals of escalation, conflict, or unpredictability. Even neutral situations can be flagged as potential risk if ambiguity is high enough to suggest possible harm or instability.

Under stress, avoidance becomes the dominant response pattern. The system prioritizes leaving, distancing, or minimizing exposure rather than engaging directly with the source of instability.

In social environments, this manifests as withdrawal from conflict, avoidance of confrontation, and reduced engagement in escalating situations. The system maintains awareness but reduces direct participation.

In environmental contexts, instability is managed through repositioning rather than correction. The system prioritizes safety through distance and observation rather than intervention.

At its most extreme expression, this becomes chronic avoidance, where even necessary confrontation is bypassed due to perceived risk of instability.

Internal Presentation

Internally, this state operates through threat prediction and risk amplification. The system evaluates environments for potential escalation before engagement occurs.

External Presentation

Externally, this appears as withdrawal, hesitation, or disengagement from unstable or high-pressure situations.

Influence Layer

Influence manifests as avoidance fielding. Environments shift as participation decreases, reducing direct interaction with unstable dynamics.
