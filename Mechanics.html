Animality: Core Mechanics

Animality is a personality typology that maps the automatic, split-second reflexes that happen in within ourselves before our rational minds can process the situation. It focuses entirely on unconscious, pre-rational survival instincts under stress.

How To Type & How Does It Function?

The Trigger

Triggers focus on a single principle: the sudden disruption of your safety baseline. Whether the threat comes from chaotic surroundings or interpersonal tension, the subconscious bypasses logical analysis. It registers that the environment is unsafe and instantly signals the core to react.

The Inner Core >in< (Exactly One)

This is the root of your type. It represents your absolute, raw internal need when stress hits. It is a pure, unyielding instinctual drive that operates entirely below conscious thought and never changes.

The Outer Translation <ex> (Up to Two)

This is your automatic behavioral defense. Because different environments require different reactions, the subconscious can develop up to two distinct Outer behaviors to protect that single Core need. The body automatically deploys whichever behavior fits the specific threat.

Note: Any reaction that falls entirely outside your designated Core or Outer behaviors is a Behavioral Mishap—an erratic, unexpected reaction that occurs when you are too emotionally exhausted to cope normally.

The Liminal State <lm<

This is a state of zero translation. When you are Liminal, your outer translation doesn't exist. Without a filter to hide your impulses, you act exactly as your core demands. It is raw, unmasked instinct brought exactly as is.

The Influence {} (Optional Modifier)

An optional final layer that acts as a filter. It does not change your Core, but it slightly reshapes the expression of your Outer behaviors, adding a specific nuance to how you carry out those defenses.

Contradictory Typing

A complete type profile is written using the syntax: <ex>Outer(s) (separated via "/") >in<Inner(optionally {influence}) or <lm<Liminal (again, optional {Influence}).

Your formula can feature animals that pull in the same direction, opposite directions, or work together. However, the system strictly forbids combinations that are unrelated yet operate in the same directional plane.

The body cannot execute two completely different versions of the same physical movement at the exact same time. If a formula attempts to pair two unrelated methods of doing the same broad action (such as two completely different, conflicting styles of avoidance, or two completely different styles of forward hostility), the behaviors don't make sense. These pairings are mechanically impossible within the framework.
